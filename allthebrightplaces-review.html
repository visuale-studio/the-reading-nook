ALL THE BRIGHT PLACES

rom com · review

all the bright places is one of those rare books that manages to hold joy and devastation in the same hand without dropping either. it begins with two people meeting at the edge of a bell tower — theodore finch, a boy who thinks about death more often than he would like to admit, and violet, a girl still quietly drowning in grief following the loss of her beloved sister. their connection begins tentatively and under the raised eyebrows of everyone around them, but what grows between them is something so tender and so alive that it feels like a small, precious miracle. jennifer niven takes these two deeply wounded people and lets them heal each other in the most beautifully imperfect way — through wandering, through wonder, through the kind of honesty that only exists between two people who have nothing left to hide. the writing is luminous and achingly real, capturing both the electric highs of first love and the crushing weight of grief and mental illness with equal compassion and unflinching honesty. and the ending — which i will not spoil here — arrives like a quiet blow to the chest that you feel for days afterward. a book that is as devastating as it is beautiful, and absolutely necessary.
